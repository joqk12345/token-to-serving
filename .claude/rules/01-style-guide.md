# Style Guide

Write precise, concrete, engineering-minded technical nonfiction.

## Voice

Use a patient senior-engineer voice: direct, specific, and careful about assumptions.

The book should feel like a guided system design investigation, not a lecture transcript.

## Use

- small numerical examples before general formulas;
- named bottlenecks: compute, memory, bandwidth, communication, scheduling;
- diagrams that expose data movement;
- tradeoff language;
- source conditions around performance claims;
- short conclusions after dense technical passages.

## Avoid

- marketing prose;
- unsupported "optimal", "best", "always", "solves";
- acronym cascades before definitions;
- slide-bullet prose;
- explaining every paper as a separate island;
- hiding uncertainty behind confident verbs.

## Chapter Rhythm

The default movement:

1. Show the concrete pain.
2. Reduce it to a minimal example.
3. Introduce the math or algorithm.
4. Expose the system bottleneck.
5. Explain the design.
6. State what the design buys and what it costs.
7. Give the reader a reusable test or rule of thumb.

The movement is required; the visible headings may vary.
